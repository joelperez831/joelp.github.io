Sentiment Analysis on Rick and Morty:

After reading Text Mining with R, I was trying to think of a project I could apply what I had learned. It wasn’t until I came across this blog post by Tamas Szilagyi: A TIDY TEXT ANALYSIS OF RICK ANDMORTY that my eyes were opened.

As a huge Rick and Morty fan, the idea of combining text analysis and my favorite show was perfect. I did my best to make this project my own. I looked online for the subtitles and began cleaning up the text. I started with looking at the overall sentiment of the show.

## Read: 1 seasons, 31 episodes

“The answer is: Don’t think about it.”

     As many think of Rick and Morty as a very dark show. According to the sentiment analysis of the show in its entirety the shows text is actually more netural, with an almost equal amount of negative and positive sentiments.

     Let’s look at each season to see if this is the case for every season:

## [1] "Sentiment of Rick and Morty By Season: "
#Top negative and positive sentiments:
#### One thing to note is that mother is regarded as both negative and positive, hence why it is filled with two colors. My hypothesis is that it depends on the context of how mother is being used. Another interesting find is that the most common word in each season is god. The show is known for questioning the existence of god, which I was suprised to see how often the word god is actually mentioned. However, the context of how god is usually used as in a phrase like: “Oh my god”, which is not usually a good thing. Nevertheless, this could be why the overall sentiment analysis of Rick and Morty might be a misrepresentation. Will look more into this later.

Number of times each character is mentioned per season:

#### It’s not suprising that the most mentioned characters of Rick and Morty are Rick and Morty. However, we can see Morty was the real star of the first season. He was mentioned about twice as much as Rick and after the first season, he dropped all the way down. Justin Roiland, the voice of Rick and Morty, said he felt Rick saying “Morty” was a verbal crutch and wanted to try to get rid of it. As we can see by the graph, he really did follow through.

Term frequencies are a large part in text mining. So, let’s plot the top 20

words per season and exclude main character names and common words.

episodeWords <- df %>% unnest_tokens(word, Text) %>% anti_join(
rbind(data.frame(word = c("xhmikosr", "www.addic7ed.com", "sync",
"corrections"), lexicon = "custom"))) %>% count(season_num, word, sort = TRUE) %>% ungroup()
totalWords <- episodeWords %>% group_by(season_num) %>% summarize(total =
sum(n))
episodeWords <- left_join(episodeWords, totalWords)
episodeWords <- episodeWords %>% bind_tf_idf(word, season_num, n)
plotWords <- episodeWords %>% arrange(desc(tf_idf)) %>% mutate(word =factor(word, levels = rev(unique(word))))
plotWords %>% top_n(20) %>% ggplot(aes(word, tf_idf, fill = as.factor(season_num))) + geom_col() + labs(x=
NULL, y = "tf-idf") + coord_flip()

We can see from that the top positions for each season are: meeseeks, unity and pickle, which, if you watch Rick and Morty, is not so suprising. One thing I loved is the word “noob” made the list. Again, if you watch Rick and Morty, you know there is a character called, “Noob Noob”, who is one of my favorite temporary side characters on the show.

N-grams allow us to look at the relationship between words in text. From the table above, we can see the words following the word “god” and the words after the word “god”. It looks like “my god”“,”oh god" and “god damn” are the most commonly used phrases with the word god. If we assume the context of these phrases to be stricktly negative, which as an avid fan, I can say that they are definitely used more in a negative context and if we treat them as such, let’s see how that changes the overall sentiment of the show.
